Punjab, the northern state of India, is known for its food as well as its festivals and dances. Punjab represents five rivers, Jhelum, Chenab, Ravi, Beas and Sutlej. Where these five rivers meet. The land of Punjab has a rich cultural history of celebrating various festivals and fairs with grandeur and enthusiasm. The festivals of Punjab are famous for their vibrancy and colour. In Punjab, the festival of Lohri is mainly celebrated in the beginning of the year and in the joy of harvest, in which women and men celebrate by circling the fire. Punjab is an agricultural state. Here the festival of Baisakhi is celebrated with great pomp, showing the harvesting of crops. In Punjab, Diwali, Dussehra and the birthday of Guru Nanak are celebrated with great enthusiasm and enthusiasm.

Hola Mohalla Holi is one of the most famous festivals of Punjab. The Sikh community of Punjab celebrates the 'Festival of Colours'. In the state of Punjab this festival is famous as 'Hola Mohalla' and is celebrated a day after the festival of Holi. This festival also provides them an opportunity to showcase their martial arts specially 'Wrestling'. The fun in the festivals here is also defined in different ways. The festival is held at Anandpur Sahib and is a tradition that was started by Guru Gobind Singh in the 1700s. The festival is marked by sports like archery, horse riding, fencing etc.

Baisakhi is one of the most important festivals of Punjab, celebrated in the first days of April or May as the new year according to the Saka calendar. It is considered one of the biggest harvest festivals in the North-Western region. This special day was celebrated by the tenth Guru of the Sikhs, known as Guru Gobind Singh, who founded the Khalsa in the year 1699. On this day Sikhs go to Gurdwaras and listen to Kirtans. After the religious rites and traditions are over, sweet semolina is served to the general public. The ceremony ends with 'langar' or community lunch. Mock duets and bands playing religious tunes form part of the procession. This festival is also marked as the last opportunity to rest before starting the harvesting of corn.

Lohri is celebrated just before the day of Makar Sankranti in the state of Punjab and is one of the most famous festivals of Punjab. The festival of Lohri is a very special festival for the community of Punjabis. This auspicious and joyous festival celebrates fertility and the spark of life. Religious rites and traditions are celebrated with great devotion. All the locals gather sweets, puffed rice and popcorn amidst the flames to throw a bonfire. They indulge themselves in the gathering by singing songs and exchanging greetings. The first Lohri of the newly wed bride and newborn child is very important.

Guruparv is celebrated by Sikhs to commemorate their Gurus. There are 2 major Gurpurabs held during the whole year. The first Gurpurab is held in the month of Kartika or in the month of October or November to commemorate the teachings of the founder of Sikhism. The second Gurpurab is held in the month of Pusa or in the month of December/January to commemorate the birth of Guru Gobind Singh. These days the Guru Granth Sahib is taken out in a procession in which the local people participate with great enthusiasm. The martyrdom of Guru Tegh Bahadur and Guru Arjun Dev is also observed on both the days of this special festival. Non-stop religious discourses and recitation of texts are held. Free meals known as 'langar' are also served to rich and poor alike.

Tika is celebrated in the month of Kartik as per Hindu calendar or in the month of October/November according to English calendar. Mahila-loka applied saffron and rice grains on her brother's forehead to remove the evils from his life. They also wear fine attire to perform auspicious ceremonies. They also sing while feeding their brothers homemade sweets and pray for their long life and prosperity. In return the brothers also give them some gifts or money as a token of love and affection.
