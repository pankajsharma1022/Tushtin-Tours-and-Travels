With a rich and vibrant cultural heritage, the festivals of Nagaland are celebrated with great enthusiasm among the residents of this north eastern state. In Nagaland, there are about 16 tribes including Kukis, Angamis, Chang, Konyaks, Aos, Kacharis, Sumis, Chakeshang, Lothas, Pochuris and others. Each of these tribes has its own unique indigenous culture, customs, tradition and festivals. The purpose of the festivals of Nagaland is to promote inter-tribal interaction. Since agriculture is one of the main aspects of the economy of the state, many festivals of Nagaland revolve around agriculture. The major theme of all the festivals of Nagaland having different names in different Naga dialects remains the offering of prayers to the One Supreme. The village headman preaches the spirit of the gods with sacrifices for bountiful yields of crops.

Hornbill Festival is one of the biggest and important festivals of North Eastern India between 1st to 10th December at the Naga Heritage Village of Kisma near Kohima in Nagaland. The festival is organized by the state tourism along with the art and culture departments and showcases the diversity in their many cultural performances. The festival is named after the hornbill bird because the bird is symbolic in the native folklore and culture of tribal communities. Hornbill Festival was started in the year 2000 as a main event to promote tourism in Nagaland and unite all the tribes to share a platform under one roof. The week-long festivities offer tourists an incredible opportunity to experience a variety of local traditions, handicrafts, folk songs, dances, traditional Naga Morang exhibition and other customs of Nagaland. Apart from these, a major highlight of the festival is the Hornbill International Rock Festival, held at the Indira Gandhi Stadium, where both local and international rock bands are equally represented.

Bamboo Day Celebrations: One of the popular festivals of Nagaland, Bamboo Day Celebration has international significance as it is celebrated globally as well. Nagaland is home to about 5 percent of India's growing stock of bamboo. The bamboo and cane crafts of Nagaland have excelled in the present era as they are rich in the tradition of this craft. The main objective of the Bamboo Day celebration is to raise awareness about the use of bamboo in a person's daily life and art. Stalls are set up during the festival to display bamboo art and other related products.

Tokhu Imong Festival Celebrated by Lotha Nagas, who are known as one of the major tribes of the state, Tokhu Imong a harvest celebrated for a period of about 9 days in the month of November After that there is a festival. The festival not only promotes unity among the carcass but also promotes inter-tribal harmony and cooperation to all. The festival is marked with feasting, merry making, drinking, playing games, singing folk songs and dancing.

Yamshe Festival is a pre-harvest festival which is also a major festival of Pochari tribe. Celebrated in October, this festival is celebrated in 2 phases, namely Badi Yamashe and Chhoti Yamashe. Big yamshe is all around preparation time as the village youth clean up the entire village. The purification of the house and a ritual feast characterize the miniature yamshe. Apart from these, some other festivals of Nagaland include Tuluni, Metemano, Monu, Miu, Bushu and Nazu etc. All these festivals are celebrated with great enthusiasm and enthusiasm.

Mim Kut Festival is a religious festival which is celebrated after the harvesting of crops which is celebrated with great fanfare because of the beliefs of the people. One of the primary reasons why the Kuki tribe celebrates this festival is to express their success on the harvest of maize, which is also regarded as the last crop of the season.

Innumerable tourists from all over the country and abroad come to see the Hega Festival. The main attraction of this festival is the traditional song and dance here. Also, the competition for eating Naga chillies in this festival also attracts the attention of the people.
