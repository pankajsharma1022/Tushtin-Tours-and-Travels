Hardly a month goes by in Rajasthan in which there is no religious festival. The most notable and special festival is Gangaur, in which clay idols of Mahadev and Parvati are worshiped by women of all castes for 15 days, and later they are immersed in water. The priests and officers also participate in the procession of immersion and the procession comes out with musical instruments. Both Hindus and Muslims participate in each other's festivals. These occasions are full of enthusiasm and gaiety. Another major festival takes place in Pushkar near Ajmer, which is a mixed form of religious festival and cattle fair. Here farmers from all over the state bring their camels and cows, buffaloes etc., and pilgrims come in search of salvation. The shrine of Sufi spiritualist Khwaja Moinuddin Chishti in Ajmer is one of the holiest shrines for Muslims in India. On the occasion of Urs, about three lakh devotees from all over the country and abroad visit the dargah every year. dance drama The typical dance of Rajasthan is Ghoomar, which is performed only by women during festive occasions. Gher dance (performed by men and women), Panihari (elegant dance of women), and Kachi Ghodi (in which male dancers are seated on a Vanvati mare) are also popular. The most famous song is 'Khurja', which tells the story of a woman. who wants to send a message to her husband through the bird Khurja and promises him a prized prize in return for his service. Rajasthan has contributed to Indian art and has a literary tradition, especially of Bhat poetry. Chandbardai's poetry Prithviraj Raso or Chand Rasa, whose earliest manuscripts date back to the 12th century, is a popular medium of entertainment with the Khyal, a dance-drama and whose poetry themes are based on festivities, history or romance. Ancient rarities are abundant in Rajasthan, including Buddhist inscriptions, Jain temples, forts, magnificent princely palaces and mosques and domes.
